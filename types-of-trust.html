In this glossary specific offshore structures are marked with an asterisk.

Bare trust

This is sometimes known as a simple trust. The beneficiary has an immediate and absolute right to the capital and income held in trust. The assets are held in the name of a trustee. The bare trust is commonly used to transfer assets to minors.

Charitable trust

This trust does not have beneficiaries; it exists for some social benefit. It may have characteristics of other trusts, depending on the jurisdiction, and typically has favourable tax status.

Discretionary trust

The beneficiaries and entitlements to the fund are determined by the settlor and set out in the trust instrument; the trustees typically manage the funds in accordance with the settlorâ€™s guidelines.

Fixed trust

The beneficiary's entitlement is fixed by the settlor. The trustee has little or no discretion. Common examples are a trust for a child, a life interest (to provide an income to someone during his/her lifetime), and a remainder (to pay capital to one person upon the death of another).

Hybrid trust

This combines elements of the fixed and the discretionary trust. The trustee is legally obliged to pay a fixed amount of the trust assets to each beneficiary, as agreed by the settlor. The trustee has discretion over the remaining assets within the framework set by the settlor.

Incentive trust

Trustees adhere to fixed conditions for distributing income/assets, as set by the settlor to influence the beneficiaryâ€™s behaviour.

Interest in possession trust

The beneficiary has an immediate and automatic right to the income from the trust, or to enjoy its assets in the present (such as the right to live in a trust property). The trustee must pass all income received, less expenses, to the beneficiary.

Irrevocable trust

The terms of the trust cannot be amended or terminated without permission of the beneficiary, effectively removing rights of ownership from the settlor.

Protective trust

This is used in estate planning to protect property from possession by creditors prior to inheritance. The current owner continues to inhabit the property but, upon his/her death ownership transfers to the inheritor (not to any creditor).

*Purpose trust

The non-charitable purpose trust, like the charitable trust, has no beneficiaries. It exists for some non-charitable purpose. It is not usually permitted onshore, but can be set up in certain offshore jurisdictions (Bahamas, Bermuda, British Virgin Islands, Cayman Islands).

Revocable trust

This may be altered or revoked by the settlor at any time, and, with minimal administrative costs, may act as an alternative to a will.

*Self-Settled Spendthrift Trust

This is a type of spendthrift trust in which the trust creator is also the sole beneficiary of the trust. It is sometimes referred to as a self-designated trust. It is available offshore only, and is a tax-planning tool. It can also be used as a way to avoid debt as there is a greater burden of proof on creditors. This type of trust is therefore more heavily regulated and available in fewer jurisdictions.

Spendthrift trust

*This form of discretionary trust allows the trustee to spend trust income on behalf of a beneficiary who is unable to control his/her spending.

*STAR trusts (Cayman Islands) and VISTA trusts (British Virgin Islands)

These jurisdictions offer specialised forms of the discretionary trust which permit the trustees to remain neutral with regard to the management of any company that forms part of the trust. STAR and VISTA trusts tend to be used by hedge funds and in bond issues respectively; they are recognised under the Hague Convention.

Unit trust

The beneficiaries (unit holders) each possess a certain share (number of units) and can instruct the trustee to pay money to them out of the trust property by redeeming their units. It is a vehicle for collective investment - the person providing the assets to the trustee is also the beneficiary. Unit trusts are always discretionary.

 

 

A note on the rule against perpetuities (royal lives clause): trusts are generally subject to this rule which limits the time within which trust property must be distributed. Some offshore jurisdictions have modified their laws to allow for greater flexibility, and some have abolished the rule.
